# 📊 04 — Key Insights
### KJ Somaiya College of Engineering | Internship Trends Analysis (2018–2025)
> Derived from 2,004 internship records across 7 academic years — CS/COMP branch

---

## 1. 📈 Overall Participation Trend

| Academic Year | Total Internships |
|---|---|
| 2018 – 19 | 182 |
| 2019 – 20 | 263 |
| 2020 – 21 | **466** *(peak)* |
| 2021 – 22 | 389 |
| 2022 – 23 | 327 |
| 2023 – 24 | 279 |
| 2024 – 25 | 98 *(partial year)* |

**Insight:** Internship participation peaked in **2020–21** — counterintuitively, the height of the COVID-19 pandemic. This spike is likely explained by a surge in **in-house and online internships** as students and institutions adapted to remote-first environments, with faculty-mentored internal projects being recorded as internships. Post-2021, participation has been on a consistent downward trend year-over-year, suggesting that the post-COVID normalisation has not translated back into increased external industry placements.

---

## 2. 💰 Paid vs Unpaid Internship Trend

| Academic Year | Paid | Unpaid | Paid % |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2018 – 19 | 8 | 174 | 4.4% |
| 2019 – 20 | 26 | 237 | 9.9% |
| 2020 – 21 | 74 | 392 | 15.9% |
| **2021 – 22** | **93** | **296** | **23.9% *(peak)*** |
| 2022 – 23 | 75 | 252 | 22.9% |
| 2023 – 24 | 48 | 231 | 17.2% |
| 2024 – 25 | 10 | 88 | 10.2% |

**Insight:** Paid internship percentage peaked in **2021–22 at 23.9%** and has been declining every year since — dropping to just **10.2% in 2024–25**, a near **57% relative decline** from the peak. This is the single most critical finding in the dataset. Despite students gaining more experience over time, the industry is offering fewer stipend-bearing opportunities — pointing to a structural tightening in the entry-level internship market post-COVID recovery.

---

## 3. 🏢 External vs In-House Internships

| Type | Count | Paid % |
|---|---|---|
| External | 1,252 | **26.5%** |
| In-house | 752 | **0.3%** |

**Insight:** Nearly **100% of in-house internships are unpaid** — only 2 out of 752 recorded any stipend. External internships, by contrast, have a 26.5% paid rate. This reveals that the quality of financial opportunity is almost entirely dependent on whether the student secures an industry placement vs an internal college project. The large volume of in-house internships (37.5% of all records) significantly drags down the overall paid internship average.

---

## 4. 🗓️ Seasonal Distribution

| Season | Internships | Paid % |
|---|---|---|
| Summer (May – Aug) | 1,379 (68.8%) | 19.3% |
| Winter (Nov – Jan) | 629 (31.4%) | 11.6% |

**Top months:** JUN–JUL, JUN–AUG, JUL–AUG, DEC–JAN, MAY–JUL

**Insight:** The **EVEN semester (summer)** dominates internship activity, accounting for nearly **70% of all internships** — with June–July being the single most concentrated window. Summer internships also have a significantly **higher paid rate (19.3%)** compared to winter (11.6%), suggesting that industry partners offer more structured and compensated opportunities during the summer cycle. The **ODD semester (winter)** still contributes meaningfully (597 internships) but is predominantly unpaid and shorter in duration.

---

## 5. ⏱️ Duration Analysis

| Metric | All Internships | External | In-house |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mean Duration | 114 days | 82 days | 167 days |
| Median Duration | 66 days | 60 days | 106 days |
| Min | 1 day | 1 day | 1 day |
| Max | 7,693 days* | 579 days | 7,693 days* |

*outlier — likely a data entry error*

**Insight:** The **median internship duration is 66 days (~2.2 months)**, which aligns with the dominant June–July and June–August windows. However, the mean is pulled significantly higher by long-duration in-house projects (some recorded at implausible lengths). External internships average a more industry-consistent **60 days median**. Duration alone does not correlate with pay — the highest-duration internships are predominantly in-house and unpaid.

## 6. 🎓 Year of Study Analysis

| Year of Study | Count | Paid % |
|---|---|---|
| FY (1st Year) | 220 | 5.9% |
| SY (2nd Year) | 651 | 13.5% |
| TY (3rd Year) | 959 | **20.5%** |
| LY (4th Year) | 174 | **20.7%** |

**Insight:** As expected, paid internship rates improve with academic seniority. **Third and final year students have ~3.5x higher paid rates than first-year students**, reflecting the value that industry places on deeper technical exposure. First-year students (FY) at just 5.9% paid rate are almost entirely engaged in in-house, introductory projects — which is appropriate but underscores the importance of building external connections early.

## 7. 💸 Stipend Distribution (Among Paid Internships Only)

| Stipend Range | Count |
|---|---|
| Below ₹5,000 | 131 (39.2%) |
| ₹5,000 – ₹10,000 | 70 (21.0%) |
| ₹10,000 – ₹25,000 | 73 (21.9%) |
| ₹25,000 – ₹50,000 | 29 (8.7%) |
| Above ₹50,000 | 31 (9.3%) |

**Median Stipend: ₹10,000/month | Mean Stipend: ₹17,701/month**

**Insight:** Even among the 334 paid internships, **over 39% pay below ₹5,000** — which is negligible for students in a city like Mumbai. Only **18% of paid internships** offer above ₹25,000/month, which represents genuinely competitive compensation. The high mean (₹17,701) relative to median (₹10,000) suggests a small number of very high-paying internships (max: ₹1,25,000) are skewing the average — the typical paid experience is far more modest than the mean implies.

---

## 8. 🦠 COVID-19 Impact — The Full Picture

| Period | Observation |
|---|---|
| Pre-COVID (2018–20) | Low participation, very low paid rates (4–10%) |
| COVID Peak (2020–21) | Highest participation (466) driven by in-house surge |
| Post-COVID Recovery (2021–22) | Highest paid % (23.9%) — industry reopening |
| Post-Recovery Decline (2022–25) | Consistent drop in both participation and paid rates |

**Insight:** The COVID-19 pandemic created a two-phase distortion. In the short term (2020–21), it inflated raw internship numbers through in-house alternatives. In the medium term (2021–22), pent-up industry demand briefly lifted paid rates to their all-time high. But from 2022 onwards, a sustained decline in both volume and quality of internships suggests that the **post-COVID hiring correction has hit entry-level and intern pipelines harder than senior roles** — a trend consistent with broader industry layoffs and hiring freezes in the technology sector during 2022–2024.
